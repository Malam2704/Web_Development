The Paranoia

Am I being affected?

In 2016, in an attempt to sow discord and influence the United States presidential election, Russian operatives played a major role in spreading disinformation on social media.

Much of the election disinformation EIP has tracked so far originates in conspiratorial corners of the right-wing media ecosystem. “What we’re seeing right now are essentially seeds being planted, dozens of seeds each day, of false stories,” says Emerson Brooking, a resident fellow at the Atlantic Council’s Digital Forensic Research Lab, which is part of EIP. “They’re all being planted such that they could be cited and reactivated … after the election” by groups attempting to delegitimize the result by claiming the vote was unfair or manipulated.

What else are they doing?

Yochai Benkler, co-director of the Berkman Klein Center for Internet and Society at Harvardanalyzed more than 55,000 online news stories, 5 million tweets, and 75,000 posts on public Facebook pages between March and August. They found that most spikes in media coverage and social media activity on the topic were driven by Trump himself—either through his own hyperactive Twitter account, press briefings, or appearances on the Fox TV network. “Donald Trump has perfected the art of harnessing mass media to disseminate and reinforce his disinformation campaign,” the researchers write in a preprint posted earlier this month.

The effects on the election

Let's look at some stats!

To start off, a conservative media personality shared pictures that 100 votes in sonoma county CA, dumped 1000 mail in votes and the poll workers tried to cover it up but The pictures revealed empty envelopes from the 2018 election, which had been collected for recycling, according to Sonoma county officers. The ballots for the general election this year have not yet been mailed. Even so, over 25,000 Twitter users, including Donald Trump Jr., who has 5.7 million followers, posted a version of the fake ballot-dumping story within a single day.

“This narrative that you’re not going to be able to trust the election results is really problematic,” says Kate Starbird, a crisis informatics researcher at the University of Washington’s Center for an Informed Public.

But what is being done to prevent all this?

EIP works with social media platforms to help them refine their policies and explain them so that they can respond to misinformation more quickly. Several businesses have taken recent steps to flag or delete information, or make it harder to share, if long overdue, steps experts say are welcome. (Some platforms often aim to drive users into healthier behaviors, such as the recent Twitter experiment with prompts that appear when someone attempts to share a connection to an article they have not opened, urging them to read it before sharing it first.)

It won't be easy to quantify the effect of misinformation on the election. However, some hints may come from a research partnership with Facebook aimed at studying the effect of the platform on the election this year. The company provided access to data on the Facebook behavior of a large number of users who consented to be involved to 17 academic researchers. (Facebook plans to volunteer between 200,000 and 400,000 users.) Participants agree to respond to surveys and, in some cases, to leave Facebook for a period of time before the election to help researchers examine the impact on political attitudes and actions of the use of Facebook.
